What we shared in May
6 May
Why US equities have rallied this quarter

Three forces behind the recovery to record highs: 2026 earnings expectations revised up nearly 5 percentage points since the Middle East conflict began, led by technology and energy; an energy shock materially milder than 2022 in real terms; and a historically attractive valuation entry point in technology following the SaaS and Iran conflict selloffs. Key risks remain narrow earnings leadership and a forward PE of 20.9x.

15 May
CBA, banks and the Federal Budget

CBA's softer third quarter update, with rising arrears and higher impairments, landed alongside Federal Budget property tax reforms, triggering the bank's largest ever single-day fall of 10.4%. The Vantage Australian Equities Portfolio remains underweight the banks and CBA, with Airlie running a significant sector underweight.

26 May
A narrow US rally, rising yields and extreme sentiment

Five mega-cap names drove over half of US index gains since April, while global long-term yields broke multi-decade highs mid-month. Survey data showed the largest single-month jump in equity allocations on record, a crowded semiconductor trade, and an equity risk premium still consistent with fair value.

Macro

What moved markets in May

A month of narrow leadership and sharp intra-month swings: US equities extended their AI-led run to fresh records, the RBA hiked early before soft data shifted rate expectations, and a sharp oil price decline took some heat out of the inflation story.

Equities

Global equities rallied to record highs on exceptional US earnings. The ASX 200 added 1.2% but lagged global peers.

Rates

RBA hiked to 4.35%. Long yields spiked mid-month, then rallied sharply. Australian 10-year ended ~20bp lower at 4.83%.

Credit

Spreads tightened as risk appetite returned and default fears eased, supporting credit exposures.

Commodities

Brent crude fell 19% to US$92. Copper, aluminium and coking coal strengthened, lifting the miners.

Inflation & Policy

April CPI 4.2%. Softer jobs data shifted RBA expectations towards a June pause. Fed cut timing continues to be pushed out.

Month in review

Global equities led portfolio returns, with the Global Equities sleeve gaining +5.3%, driven by strong outperformance from Arrowstreet Hedged (+7.5%) and T. Rowe Price (+6.0%). The Vantage Direct Australian Equities component also outperformed its index, led by the overweight to resources (BHP, RIO, ORI, MND) alongside the underweight to banks, which weakened during the month. Fixed income posted modest gains as bonds rallied in the second half of May, with returns led by Bentham ABS (+1.5%) and Bentham Global Income (+1.3%). Property and infrastructure was the only sleeve to detract, as the mid-month spike in long yields weighed on rate-sensitive assets. Growth and Moderate Growth outperformed their Morningstar benchmarks for the month.

Since inception (14 July 2025)

Despite heightened volatility this year — including sharp drawdowns in March and the mid-May yield spike — all three multi-sector portfolios are on track for solid financial year returns, currently ranging between 6.5% and 8.0% since inception.

Market

The ASX 200 returned 1.2% for May, closing at 8,731.7, masking significant volatility with eight sessions moving more than 1% in either direction. Performance was sharply barbelled: Materials surged 10.5% on broad commodity strength, with BHP up 16.0% the single largest index contributor, while Financials fell 3.9% as CBA's softer quarterly update and the Budget's proposed property tax reforms combined to drive the bank's largest ever single-day fall. Health Care was the weakest sector, driven by CSL down 22%, and Energy declined 5.9% as oil retreated. Value outperformed growth by 4.7%, and small caps outpaced large.

Vantage Direct Australian Equities

The portfolio returned 1.48%, ahead of the ASX 300's 1.25%. The overweight to resources (BHP +16.0%, Rio Tinto +10.9%, Orica +10.3%, Monadelphous +11.1%) was the primary contributor, while the structural underweight to major banks, particularly CBA, cushioned the Financials weakness. Energy holdings (Origin -10.2%, Woodside -8.6%) and Healthcare names (ResMed -3.2%) detracted. The FYTD result of 2.32% versus 5.53% reflects stock-specific headwinds in CSL, Xero and Treasury Wine Estates, which offset strong contributions from BHP, Newmont and Rio Tinto.

Market

Global equities extended their run higher in May, with the MSCI World returning 4.5% in AUD terms (unhedged). Regional leadership was broad but uneven: Japan was the standout, up 11.9% in local currency, as export earnings benefited from yen weakness and accelerating AI-related semiconductor demand. Emerging markets returned 9.5%, with Taiwan and Korea among the strongest performers. The US returned 5.3%, again led by mega-cap technology, while Europe (+2.9%) and the UK were more modest. Australia lagged all major regions at 1.0%.

Factor performance was pronounced. Momentum led all factors with a gain of 4.9%, and Composite Growth added 4.3%, reflecting the concentration of returns in high-growth technology names. Quality (-1.6%) and Low Volatility (-2.5%) were the weakest factors, while Value (-1.0%) also underperformed — consistent with the momentum and growth-dominated environment.

Our managers

The portfolio returned 5.30%, ahead of the blended benchmark's 4.64%. Arrowstreet's hedged global alpha extension strategy led with a +7.5% gain, benefiting from its systematic momentum and factor tilts. T. Rowe Price Global Equity returned +6.0%, ahead of benchmark, as its growth orientation aligned with the month's leadership. Quality and value-oriented strategies posted positive but more modest returns: Aoris (+1.6%), Vanguard Global Value (+1.7%) and VanEck MSCI International Quality (+3.6%) all lagged the benchmark, consistent with the factor headwinds. FYTD the portfolio is well ahead of the blended benchmark at +16.14% versus +14.79%, driven by Arrowstreet's exceptional FYTD performance.

Market

Rate-sensitive assets were under sustained pressure through most of the month as long bond yields spiked to multi-decade highs before the late-month dovish repricing. The two benchmark indices tell the story clearly: the FTSE Developed Core Infrastructure index fell 1.43% as utilities, energy infrastructure and transport faced headwinds from higher discount rates. Global listed property was more resilient; the FTSE EPRA Nareit Developed index fell only 0.56%, as the late-month bond rally and renewed rate optimism partly offset earlier losses in REITs.

Our managers

The portfolio returned -0.78%, ahead of the blended benchmark's -0.99%. Atlas Infrastructure (hedged) was the most resilient, down only 0.4%, reflecting its diversified exposure to regulated utility and transportation assets. Quay Global Real Estate (unhedged) fell 1.0%, weighed by its significant European and UK REIT holdings. Ausbil Global Essential Infrastructure (hedged) was the principal detractor, declining 2.9% as its energy infrastructure and utilities exposures bore the brunt of the yield spike. FYTD the portfolio has returned 9.81%, with Atlas (+23.5% FYTD) and Ausbil (+17.4%) the standout contributors, while Quay's unhedged positioning in a year of rising global yields has been the primary drag. The portfolio trails the blended benchmark FYTD (-3.63%), largely reflecting Quay's underperformance relative to the hedged property index.

Market

A volatile month in two acts. Long yields broke to multi-decade highs mid-month — the Australian 10-year touching above 5%, a 15-year high, the US 30-year reaching its highest level since 2007 — before soft jobs data and growing RBA pause expectations drove a sharp rally. The Australian 10-year finished ~20bp lower at 4.83%, handing duration-heavy benchmarks a strong month. Credit spreads tightened as risk appetite returned, with investment grade spreads compressing across the curve.

Our managers

The portfolio returned 0.88%, a solid absolute result that lagged the blended benchmark's 1.22% gain, consistent with the portfolio's materially shorter average duration of 3.67 years versus both benchmark indices. Credit exposures performed well as spreads tightened: Bentham Asset Backed Securities returned +1.5%, benefiting from spread compression across the structured credit market, and Bentham Global Income returned +1.3%, reflecting credit income and modest rate exposure. Macquarie Dynamic Bond and Perpetual Diversified Income benefited from the second-half yield rally. Coolabah Short Term Income, with its minimal duration, posted a more muted return consistent with its mandate.

The same short-duration positioning explains the standout FYTD story: the portfolio's 2.79% is materially ahead of the blended benchmark's 1.31% in a financial year dominated by rising yields. A formal review of the fixed income manager line-up is currently underway, and changes will be communicated to advisers ahead of any implementation.
