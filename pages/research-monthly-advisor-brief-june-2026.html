Key market events in June
12 June
Oil collapses as the US and Iran reach a peace framework

A framework agreement to end the conflict and reopen the Strait of Hormuz sent Brent crude down 20.9% for the month to US$72.9/bbl, its worst month since the depths of the pandemic. The same week, SpaceX's US$86bn debut became the largest IPO in history, kicking off a record half-year for global equity issuance. Lower energy prices eased the near-term inflation outlook, but the disinflation this delivers is a fuel story, not yet a structural one.

18 June
A hawkish debut for new Fed chair Kevin Warsh

Warsh's first FOMC meeting held rates at 3.50–3.75% but stripped out the Fed's easing bias, with nine of eighteen officials now forecasting at least one hike this year after US inflation hit 4.2%. Markets that began June pricing cuts ended it debating hikes. The US dollar rallied 2.2% on the DXY and the AUD fell 3.7% to 0.6919, a direct tailwind for our unhedged global equities exposure.

26 June
Twelve months of resources leadership unwound in a single month

Weaker commodity prices (gold -11.7%, iron ore -6.3%, Brent -20.9%) drove Materials down 6.7% and Energy down 8.9% in June, the sharpest reversal of the year, while Health Care, Consumer Staples and Consumer Discretionary each rose over 12% in a rotation into previously underperforming areas. The Vantage Australian Equities Portfolio's resources overweight, the primary driver of its strong FY26 result, was a modest drag in June as this rotation played out.

Macro

What moved markets in June

A month defined by three forces: peace in the Gulf and a collapse in oil prices, a hawkish debut from new Fed chair Kevin Warsh, and a sharp reversal in market leadership as resources gave back a year of gains to previously unloved defensive and cyclical sectors.

Equities

Global equities were mixed as mega-cap technology pulled back. Leadership broadened to cyclicals and value. The ASX 200 rose 0.7%, closing FY26 up 6.1%.

Rates

The RBA held at 4.35% with a retained tightening bias. New Fed chair Warsh removed the Fed's easing bias in his first meeting.

Credit

Corporate bonds were resilient despite modest spread widening as the hawkish Fed pivot repriced the front end of curves.

Commodities

Brent crude fell 20.9% to US$72.9 on the Gulf peace framework. Gold fell 11.7% and iron ore 6.3%, unwinding resources leadership.

Inflation & Policy

May CPI eased to 4.0% on lower fuel prices, but trimmed mean rose to 3.6%. US CPI hit 4.2%, the hottest since 2023.

Month in review

June was a strong month for the Vantage diversified portfolios. All three finished positive, with returns rising by risk profile as the late-month rotation into cyclicals, staples and offshore assets flowed through: Moderate Growth returned 1.52%, Growth 1.68% and High Growth 1.85%. Global equities exposure was the standout contributor — the AUD fell 3.7% against the USD in June on the hawkish Fed pivot, and our meaningfully unhedged offshore exposure benefited directly, with the Vantage Global Equities Portfolio returning 3.01% for the month. Australian equities exposure was more muted at 0.42%, as the portfolio's resources overweight worked against it during the rotation into staples, healthcare and discretionary. Fixed income added a small positive contribution as yields eased on lower oil prices, while listed property and infrastructure rose 3.02% but trailed its combined benchmark. All three risk profiles remain comfortably ahead of their CPI-plus objectives on a CYTD basis, from 2.65% ahead (Moderate Growth) to 3.30% ahead (High Growth).

Since inception (14 July 2025)

Global equities was the standout sleeve for the year: the core Arrowstreet global equity strategy and the international small companies allocation were the primary drivers of a result well ahead of benchmark, with the more defensively positioned Aoris international strategy the main offset. Australian equities lagged its benchmark for the year — the core index exposure and the resources overweight, led by BHP, were strongly positive in absolute terms but could not keep pace with a benchmark dominated even more heavily by mining sector strength, while growth and quality satellite holdings including CSL, Xero and Treasury Wine Estates further weighed on the relative result. Property and infrastructure was positive for the year but behind its blended benchmark, with the infrastructure managers ATLAS Infrastructure and Ausbil Global Essential Infrastructure the strongest contributors and Quay Global Real Estate the main drag. Fixed income was the standout on a relative basis, comfortably ahead of its blended benchmark, as the portfolio's shorter duration positioning suited a year in which rates stayed higher for longer than the market initially expected.

Moderate Growth has returned 8.13% p.a. since inception, 3.26% p.a. ahead of its CPI+2% objective. Growth has returned 9.10% p.a., 3.27% p.a. ahead of CPI+3%. High Growth has returned 10.04% p.a., 3.73% p.a. ahead of CPI+3.5%. Return dispersion across risk profiles is behaving as designed heading into year two, and each portfolio now has a genuine one-year track record we can begin reporting against rolling annual benchmarks.

CFS — FY2026 financial year in review

Focusing on the Super accounts, Growth returned 6.85% for FY26 and Moderate 6.16%, both solid absolute results in a year of dispersed underlying fund performance. Within the Australian equities sleeve, Vinva Australian Equity Fund was the standout, ahead of the S&P/ASX 300 benchmark for the year, while First Sentier Concentrated Australian Share Fund was the primary drag, materially behind benchmark as its growth and technology exposure sold off on AI disruption concerns; DNR Capital also lagged. Within global equities, the CFS Hedged Index Global Share Fund and T. Rowe Price Global Equity Fund were both comfortably ahead of the MSCI World benchmark, with the hedged share class benefiting from being less exposed to the AUD's strength earlier in the year relative to the unhedged index option. Fixed income was a consistent positive, with Perpetual Diversified Income and Schroder Absolute Return Income both well ahead of the short-duration cash benchmark, while Bentham Global Income lagged. Property and infrastructure was the weakest sleeve at the total portfolio level: Quay Global Real Estate (unhedged) fell for the year against a positive unhedged property benchmark, and the CFS Index Global Listed Infrastructure Fund modestly trailed its hedged infrastructure benchmark. Pension accounts finished ahead of Super throughout the range, reflecting the absence of contributions tax drag.

June

Market

The ASX 200 rose 0.7% on a total return basis in June, closing at 8,778.7 and taking the calendar year to 2.4%. Beneath the modest headline, leadership rotated sharply. Materials fell 6.7% and Energy 8.9% as gold (-11.7%), iron ore (-6.3%) and Brent crude (-20.9%) all fell, while Health Care rose 13.3%, Consumer Staples 13.0% and Consumer Discretionary 12.2% in a broad move into previously underperforming areas. Momentum factors reversed hard in favour of quality, yield and low volatility. Small caps fell 2.0%, weighed by their resources tilt.

Vantage Direct Australian Equities

The portfolio returned 0.43% in June, modestly behind the S&P/ASX 300's 0.60%. Insurance and diversified financials holdings were the main support as quality and yield names rallied through the month, while the portfolio's resources overweight — including BHP and Rio Tinto — detracted as the sector gave back some of its recent strength. A holding in HUB24 was also a drag.

FY2026

Market

FY26 was a resources-dominated year for the ASX 200 despite June's broad-based reversal. Materials returned 47.5% and Energy 9.4% for the year, while Information Technology fell 37.2% and Health Care 37.4%. The benchmark ASX 300 returned 6.2% for the year, a return almost entirely explained by the concentrated strength in miners.

Vantage Direct Australian Equities

The portfolio returned 2.74% for FY26 against the S&P/ASX 300's 6.16%, an underperformance of 3.42%. The core index exposure combined with the overweight to resources, particularly BHP, was the primary source of the portfolio's absolute return for the year, given the strength of the resources sector. The shortfall against benchmark reflects the active satellite positions held outside the core allocation — growth and quality names such as CSL, Xero, Treasury Wine Estates, ResMed and Technology One — which provided valuable diversification away from a resources-heavy index but were a drag in a year when the index return was dominated by a narrow set of miners.

June

Market

Global shares were mixed in June, with Europe leading in local currency terms while the US drifted lower as mega-cap technology pulled back. The month was defined by divergence within the AI trade: semiconductors stayed strong on demand while the hyperscalers funding the buildout lagged. Korea's Kospi was hit by heavy foreign selling and circuit-breaker halts before recovering late in the month. Equity issuance was the other story — SpaceX raised US$86bn in the largest IPO in history and first-half US issuance topped US$250bn, a record. For Australian dollar investors, the 3.7% fall in the AUD to 0.6919 was a significant tailwind for unhedged exposures.

Our managers

The portfolio returned 3.01% in June, ahead of the 70/30 combined benchmark's 2.21%, an outperformance of 0.80%. Every underlying manager contributed positively in a strong month for offshore assets. Arrowstreet's systematic global equity strategy, which blends quantitative security selection with factor tilts, was the largest single contributor, benefiting from its exposure to the small and mid-cap names that led the month's broadening rally, and the dedicated Arrowstreet international small companies strategy performed strongly for the same reason. The Vanguard MSCI International Index ETF and T. Rowe Price's growth-oriented global equity strategy both tracked the broader market higher. The more conservatively positioned Vanguard Global Value Equity strategy and the quality-focused Aoris International Fund lagged the other managers, consistent with the month's rotation away from defensive positioning, though both still added to the result.

FY2026

Market

FY26 was an exceptional year for global equities in Australian dollar terms. The MSCI All Countries World Index returned 17.8% for the year, Japan's Nikkei 48.4% in AUD terms, and Korea's Kospi an extraordinary 129.3% in AUD terms on the back of the AI and memory chip cycle.

Our managers

The portfolio returned 19.63% for FY26, well ahead of the 70/30 combined benchmark's 17.26%, an outperformance of 2.37%. Arrowstreet's core global equity strategy was the standout performer over the year, its systematic approach capturing the breadth of the AI-driven rally across both mega-cap and smaller names, complemented by a strong year from the dedicated Arrowstreet international small companies allocation. The Vanguard MSCI International Index ETF delivered a solid index-tracking result in a strong year for global markets, while T. Rowe Price's growth bias also added meaningfully given the dominance of growth and technology leadership for much of the year. The one clear detractor was the Aoris International Fund, whose quality and valuation discipline led it to underweight the mega-cap technology names that drove much of the year's gains — a drag the rest of the portfolio more than offset.

June

Market

Listed property and infrastructure faced crosscurrents in June. Lower oil prices eased the inflation impulse, while the hawkish shift from the Fed and the RBA's tightening bias kept discount rates elevated. Global listed infrastructure and developed market REITs both posted solid gains for the month as the late-month rate backdrop stabilised.

Our managers

The portfolio returned 3.01% in June, ahead of the 50/50 combined benchmark's 2.03%, an outperformance of 0.98%. Quay Global Real Estate, the portfolio's unhedged global real estate manager, led contributions as global REITs rallied broadly through the month. Ausbil Global Essential Infrastructure and ATLAS Infrastructure, the portfolio's two hedged infrastructure managers, also added solidly as utilities and regulated infrastructure assets stabilised following earlier rate volatility. The VanEck FTSE passive property and infrastructure ETF allocations tracked their respective indices higher. All five underlying holdings were positive for the month.

FY2026

Market

FY26 was a strong year for global real assets. Global listed infrastructure (hedged) returned 17.3% for the year and developed market REITs 7.7%, while domestic AREITs lagged, down 2.2% for the year on a total return basis.

Our managers

The portfolio returned 13.12% for FY26 against the 50/50 combined benchmark's 15.75%, an underperformance of 2.63%. ATLAS Infrastructure and Ausbil Global Essential Infrastructure were the strongest contributors over the year, both benefiting from the strength in regulated utilities and infrastructure assets globally, with the VanEck FTSE Global Infrastructure ETF also adding solidly. Quay Global Real Estate was the primary drag on the year's result: its unhedged currency exposure and overweight to European and UK REITs created a headwind for much of the year, and this was the main driver of the portfolio's shortfall against the blended benchmark.

June

Market

Bond markets navigated competing forces in June. Falling oil prices eased inflation fears and supported duration, while the hawkish Fed pivot repriced the front end higher. The Australian 10-year yield fell 12 basis points to 4.7%, while the RBA held at 4.35% with a clear tightening bias retained.

Our managers

The portfolio returned 0.71% in June, marginally behind the 60/40 combined benchmark's 0.75%. Macquarie Dynamic Bond and Perpetual Diversified Income, the portfolio's core actively managed credit strategies, performed solidly through the month as credit spreads remained well behaved despite the hawkish Fed pivot. Bentham Asset Backed Securities and Bentham Global Income, the portfolio's structured credit and multi-sector credit exposures, also delivered steady income-driven returns. Coolabah Short Term Income, with its minimal duration mandate, posted a more modest result consistent with its low-volatility objective. The portfolio's overall shorter duration positioning relative to the benchmark meant it captured less of the benefit from the modest fall in yields over the month.

FY2026

Market

Australian and global bonds delivered modest positive returns for FY26 as the rate cycle plateaued. The Bloomberg AusBond Composite Index returned 1.5% for the year and the Bloomberg Global Aggregate (Hedged) 4.2%, while hybrid securities were a standout at 7.7%.

Our managers

The portfolio returned 3.52% for FY26, well ahead of the 60/40 combined benchmark's 2.07%, an outperformance of 1.45% — the strongest relative result across the single asset class range for the year. Perpetual Diversified Income and Macquarie Dynamic Bond were the strongest contributors over the year, both benefiting from active credit selection in a period of elevated income opportunities. The Bentham strategies, spanning structured credit and global multi-sector credit, also added consistently to returns. Coolabah Short Term Income's minimal duration profile meant it contributed less to absolute return but played its intended role of dampening volatility. The portfolio's shorter duration positioning relative to the benchmark was the primary driver of the year's outperformance, in a year when rates stayed higher for longer than initially expected.
